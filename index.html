Theoretical Frameworks

Three Models of Academic Literacy

Study Skills Model: Literacy is viewed as a set of discrete, transferable technical skills, such as grammar, punctuation, and spelling, applicable across contexts.

Academic Socialisation Model: Literacy is seen as contextual and disciplinary, requiring students to adopt the norms, genres, and conventions of specific academic communities.

Academic Literacies Model: Literacy is understood as socially situated and contested, involving negotiation of meaning, identity, and power within academic contexts.

The academic literacies model recognises that writing is never just about skills or socialisation, but always involves questions of power, identity, and access.

- Lea & Street (2006)

Academic Literacies Model

The academic literacies model treats reading and writing as social practices that vary with context, culture and genre. It views student writing and learning as issues of epistemology and identities rather than of skill acquisition or academic socialisation alone.

From the student point of view, a dominant feature of academic literacy practices is the requirement to switch writing styles and genres between settings, to deploy a repertoire of literacy practices appropriate to each context, and to handle the social meanings and identities each evokes.

An academic literacies perspective views student writing and learning as issues of epistemology and identities rather than of skill acquisition or academic socialisation alone, although the perspectives are not mutually exclusive and individuals may move between them according to context and purpose.

Ubuntu Concept

Ubuntu is a philosophical concept that emphasises our interconnectedness as human beings. The phrase "Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu" (I am because we are) captures the essence of this worldview, which focuses on community, mutual caring, and shared humanity.

In academic contexts, Ubuntu encourages collaborative learning, dialectic argumentation, and writing that acknowledges multiple perspectives and the communal nature of knowledge creation.

Ubuntu philosophy transforms academic literacy from individual achievement to communal knowledge-building, where writing becomes an act of relational accountability.

- Mboti (2015)

Achebe and Academic Literacy

Achebe's literary works—especially "Things Fall Apart"—seek to reclaim African stories from colonial narratives that traditionally distorted or erased African histories and cultures. He believed in helping learners see themselves reflected in the curriculum and countering colonial stereotypes.

This approach transforms academic literacy from mere skill acquisition to a process of cultural reclamation and identity formation.

Reclaiming African stories from colonial distortions, providing counter-narratives to colonial historiography, and helping learners see themselves in the curriculum challenges Eurocentric perspectives in education.

Practical Applications

Multimodal Grammar

Communication is rarely just about words. Multimodality is the concept that we create meaning using a combination of different 'modes'. Think of a webpage, a film, or even a face-to-face conversation—they all blend text, images, sounds, and gestures to convey a message. Multimodal grammar is the framework we use to understand how these different modes work together to create a coherent whole.

This approach recognises that every element—from the font of a text to the layout of a page—is a deliberate choice that contributes to the overall meaning. By understanding these choices, we become more effective communicators and more critical consumers of information in our increasingly digital world.

Meaning is constructed through various channels. Social semiotics identifies five core modes through which we communicate: linguistic, visual, aural, gestural, and spatial.

Five Modes of Communication

Linguistic: The mode of words, both written and spoken. It covers vocabulary, grammar, and the structure of sentences and paragraphs.

Visual: Everything we can see. This includes images, colors, symbols, layout, and even the font used in a text.

Aural: The mode of sound. This encompasses spoken language, music, sound effects, and the qualities of a voice like tone and pitch.

Gestural: How we use our bodies to communicate. This includes facial expressions, hand gestures, and body language.

Spatial: The arrangement of elements in space. This applies to the layout of a page, the architecture of a room, or the proximity of people in a conversation.

Meaning is constructed through various channels. Social semiotics identifies five core modes through which we communicate. Each mode offers unique potentials for making meaning, and they often work in concert to create a rich, layered message.

Multilingual Academic Literacy

Multilingual academic literacy recognises the value of diverse linguistic resources in knowledge production. It challenges monolingual biases in academia and embraces the richness that multiple languages bring to academic discourse.

This approach acknowledges that knowledge is created and communicated across languages, and that multilingual students bring unique strengths to academic contexts through their ability to navigate multiple linguistic systems.

Academic writing that embraces multilingualism recognises that all languages contain unique ways of knowing, being, and expressing knowledge.

- Canagarajah (2011)

Implementation Framework

Genre and Mode Switching

Students are required to interact with different categories of text that we define as different genres and modes. Genres are types of text, both spoken and written, such as student discussions, written notes, letters, academic essays, etc.

The shift from free flowing thoughts/ideas to explicitness in discussion with others we identified as a shift to a different genre. As Gunther Kress has pointed out, it also involves a shift of 'mode'—from internal thought to external speech.

When students shift from talk and discussion to taking notes, new requirements come into play, such as the need for explicit attention to language structure, use of headings, and use of visual as well as language "modes" such as layout.

Each genre and mode had different qualities. In their educational histories, students had not always been made explicitly aware of these qualities associated with different genres as they moved between different genres in their school work.

Academic Literacy Development

The Academic Literacy Development Programme was designed to provide additional educational opportunities for students from linguistic minority community backgrounds who would like to move on to study at university.

Students were required to interact with different categories of text defined as different genres and modes. The programme focused on developing the use of academic English in Higher Educational contexts by helping students be more aware of the different language and semiotic practices associated with the requirements of different genres in academic contexts.

As Street and Scalone noted in their analysis: "Treating such students as collaborators in the development of the academic literacies necessary for engagement with HE in the UK, can perhaps offer a different and more supportive route to 'Widening Participation' than the more traditional focus on either study skills or academic socialisation."

The ALD programme tries to challenge some of the expectations students may have met at school… about language as narrowly defined…the course involves issues of discourse, genre, writing as social process…within a notion of building on what they already had and bring to the programme rather than treating them as a deficit and just fixing that.
