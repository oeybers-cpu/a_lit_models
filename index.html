Academic Literacies Model

A framework for understanding literacy practices in academic contexts

Key Finding

"Academic literacies foreground meaning-making, identity, power, and authority in academic writing, moving beyond skills and socialization models."

Three Models of Academic Literacy

Study Skills Model:

  • Focuses on technical, transferable skills
  • Grammar, punctuation, sentence structure
  • Assumes skills apply across all contexts

Academic Socialization Model:

  • Acculturation into disciplinary discourses
  • Learning genres and conventions
  • Assumes stability in disciplinary norms

Academic Literacies Model:

  • Focus on meaning-making, identity, power
  • Recognizes literacy practices as situated
  • Embraces complexity and variability
Framework Theory

Disciplinary Literacy

Disciplinary literacy emphasizes the specialized knowledge and abilities used by experts within each discipline.

Key distinctions from content area literacy:

  • Focuses on discipline-specific practices
  • Uses unique tools of each discipline
  • Recognizes fundamental differences between disciplines
  • Addresses varied purposes, genres, and communication traditions

Research approaches:

  • Expert-novice comparisons
  • Functional linguistics analysis
  • Think-aloud protocols
Pedagogy Research

Language Across Disciplines

Vocabulary differences:

  • Science: Greek/Latin roots for precision
  • History: Metaphorical terms, perspectives
  • Mathematics: Symbolic, axiomatic language
  • Literature: Figurative, interpretive language

Grammatical patterns:

  • Science: Nominalization, passive voice
  • History: Focus on human agency, causation
  • Different points of view and attribution

Author awareness:

  • History: Author bias is central to interpretation
  • Science: Author as quality screen, not interpretive factor
  • Mathematics: Author intentionally ignored
  • Literature: Controversial role of author

Model Implications

For teaching:

  • Move beyond generic study skills
  • Address discipline-specific practices
  • Academic literacy and content area teachers connection
  • Support students' disciplinary needs

For curriculum design:

  • Recognise epistemological differences
  • Address power relations in knowledge
  • Consider institutional requirements
  • Foreground identity in writing

For student learning:

  • Develop disciplinary insider perspectives
  • Enhance critical engagement with texts
  • Support advanced interpretation skills
  • Facilitate deeper conceptual understanding
Practice Implementation

Research Foundations

Sources of disciplinary literacy:

  • History of content area reading
  • Cognitive analyses of expert readers
  • Functional linguistics research

Expert-novice studies reveal:

  • Physicists focus on unexpected information
  • Historians use sourcing, contextualization, corroboration
  • Different critical reading approaches across fields

Functional linguistics findings:

  • Science: Classification, description, nominalization
  • History: Actions, events, verbal processes
  • Different standards of precision and evidence
